A TRIBUTE TO THE NISSAN SKYLINE GT-R

THE NISSAN SKYLINE GT-R (BNR32, 1989-1994)

After cancelling the Skyline GT-R in 1973, Nissan revived the GT-R again in 1989. At the time Nissan was competing in Group A Racing with the Skyline GTS-R. Nissan wanted to retire the GTS-R in favor of a more competitive vehicle. The new generation GT-R, E-BNR32 chassis (commonly shortened to R32), was designed to dominate Group A racing.

The new 2.6 L RB26DETT-powered all-wheel-drive concept was put into production as the R32 Nissan Skyline GT-R. The R32 developed 276 hp and 361 N·m of torque, and weighed 1,430 kg. Nissan officially started its production run 21 August 1989, and began its Group A campaign in 1990. Although it was advertised as having only 276 hp due to the "Gentlemen's Agreement" in Japan, it actually had 320 hp when tested on a dyno.

THE GT-R NAMESAKE


Why GT-R? The name 'GT-R' meant "Gran Turismo Racer," which means a vehicle made for racing. The GT-R was already prevalent from 1960s to 1970s. The early GT-Rs also dominated the track, having 1,000 (yes, one thousand) victories until 1972. The first GT-Rs were very well accepted by the locals. However, due to its gasoline crisis at the time, people never chose to buy the early GT-Rs as it was a gas guzzler. It was then that in 1989, Nissan thought of reliving the legend it once had. And so, the R32 GT-R was born. Nicknamed "Godzilla" among many car enthusiasts, the R32 GT-R was a true monster in both street and in track racing.
